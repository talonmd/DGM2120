Basic Health & Care

The Basics

Chickens are hardy birds, and will do well in most environments. There are, however, some basic necessities to know before deciding to raise chickens of your own.

Food

An average hen will eat about 1/4 of a pound of food per day. That being said, the best way to feed chickens is to always have food available (called a 'full feeding method'). When hens' feed is restricted, the egg quality and ammount typically decreases. There are several types of feed you can give to your chickens (see the table below for more information).

Feed Type Description & When to Provide
Starter Feeds Starter feeds should be provided to newly hatched chicks (0-10 weeks of age). They are specifically formulated to provide the nutrients needed for the chicks to grow healthily. For meat chickens, the starter feed typically has a much higher protien content.
Grower Feeds After 10 weeks, the chickens feed should be replaced with a grower feed. They have a higher protien content (%15-%16) and are developed to promote growth through the chicks adulthood.
Layer Feeds Layer feeds are specifically created to provide the optimal amount of nutrition for laying hens. They have the added nutrient of calcium, for healthy egg shells. Hens should start to be fed this around 16-18 weeks old, or when they first begin to lay eggs. This feed will still work great for chickens who do not lay eggs, although if you are raising them for meat, a feed higher is protien is reccommended.
Scratch Scratch emcompasses feed such as cracked corn, wheat, barely, oats, etc. They do not provide as much protien, nor do they have all the balanced nutrients needed. You can think of it as a 'fast food' for chickens, or a treat that you give them on occassion. If feeding your chickens scratch, this is when it is a good idea to provide them with grit, to help their digestion.
Table Scraps While table scraps are fun to feed to your chickens, they are not nessesarily good as the sole food resource. They certainly help keep your chickens (and you) happy, and will sometimes result in richer egg yolks. If you are feeding your chickens table scraps, be sure to also provide grit so they can crush and digest the foods. One thing to keep in mind with table scraps, is that citrusy foods are not good for chickens and should be avoided.

Water

An average hen will drink about three-times as much water than food per day. It is vital to provide enough water for your chickens, especially in the hotter months of the year. It is reccomended to provide at least one quart of water per four chickens per day. A constant supply is more highly reccommended.

Supplements

Chickens might require two types of supplements: Oyster Shell (calcium), and Grit.

If a laying hen is deficient in calcium, then her eggs will have thin, or no, shells. A typical layer feed (see table below) should provide enough calcium for your layer hens. If thin shells start to become a problem (or your chickens begin eating their own shells), then oyster shell is the most commonly reccommended supplement. Mixing in about 2lbs of shell to every 100lbs of layer feed is a good ratio to keep in mind, although you can also provide a sepparate feeder with oyster shell, allowing the hens to eat as they require it.

As for grit, it is, simply put, very small rocks that the chickens can eat to help crush the foot in their gizzard, which in turn aids in their digestion. Check this webiste out if you want to read a detailed description of this process. As the website says, if grit is not provided, the chickens will be able to eat rocks from the ground, but eventually they could use most all of the available rocks. Layer pellets and crumbles are not hard for chickens to digest, but if other foods are fed, such as scratch and table scraps, then grit is needed.

Space

In order to be both healthy and happy, chickens require enough space. If your chickens do not have enough space, there will be signs such as: fighting, feather loss, pulling one anothers feathers out, and possibly sickness and cannibalism. A good reccommended amount of space per chicken is about 2 square feet in the coop, and 8-10 square feet outside of the coop (in the run). There are many differing opinions on this, but we have found that to be more than adequate. Space will also depend on the specific size of the chickens you own, and how 'free-range' you would like them to be.
